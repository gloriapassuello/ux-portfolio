Frida

A web application providing holistic menstrual cycle healthcare in form of telehealth, cycle-specific products purchase, community engagement, health metrics tracking, and learning resources.

My Role

UX Researcher, UX & UI Designer

Timeframe

5 months, part-time

View Prototype

The Problem

There is a great growth potential for women’s health technology (estimated to be worth $60 billion by 2027). Despite that, research studies show that most menstrual cycle tracking apps are inaccurate and have little health professional involvement (The Medical Futurist, 2021). Moreover it seems that companies designing technology for women rely on the ‘shrink it and pink it’ approach. As a result period trackers often show pink childish design elements and make use of gendered language where straightforward medical terminology would suffice.

Opportunities

“Cycle syncing” is a new buzzword in menstrual health (the practice of adjusting lifestyle, diet, and exercise routines to match the hormones shifts during the menstrual cycle), and more women are interested in pursuing a holistic approach to menstrual cycle health (Krupp, 2020). More about cycle syncing here

The growing concerns about data privacy and period tracking apps became a pressing issue in US after the Supreme Court has overturned the right to abortion, so that now users fear the data collected by the apps could be used against them in future criminal cases (Perez, 2022). As a result, there is a great potential for alternatives which can guarantee high data protection.

How might we create a secure and gender-neutral space to receive holistic and professional care for menstrual cycle health?

Discovery

Since I had to design a new product from scratch, a discovery phase was necessary to understand the problem space and its users, generate ideas and discover opportunities for innovation that better meet the users’ needs.

For this scope I have used a mixed methods approach using secondary and primary research methods:

  • Competitive Analysis
  • Screening Survey
  • Users' Interviews

Survey

A screener survey was created to collect information on how users keep track of their menstrual cycle health, how they support it and the impact it has on their daily life.

With the survey I could recruit participants who opted-in for interviews and it also served as a backbone for the interview script and conversation starting point with selected interviewees.

Interviews

From the screener survey results I have selected 10 participants to conduct video Interviews of 30 minutes.

Interviews were a means to understand the “WHY” for the answers in the survey and find out more about the users’ behaviours, goals, and frustrations when it comes to staying on top of their menstrual cycle health.

Participants' Profile

  • 6 nationalities
  • age range 23-37
  • symptoms impact on daily routine: low, medium, severe
  • reproductive condition: none, endometriosis, PCOS

Ideation

This phase draws from previous discovery research findings and turns the insights into personas, user journeys, and sitemap. These archetypes are the foundations that helped me creating a user-centered design solution.

Sitemap

After defining personas and user journeys I have created a sitemap for the app navigation. To verify if it was intuitive and easy to understand, I did a card sorting exercise with 8 users.

Frida’s main features are:

  • Learn about cycle phases and symptoms fixes (Home)
  • Track cycle and symptoms (Calendar)
  • View webinars, publish and read posts (Community)
  • Find healthcare experts (Care)
  • Shop cycle products (Shop)

Validation

After creating the first version of my digital wireframes it was time to start the evaluative research phase. This phase is fundamental to validate whether or not my in-progress design is effectively solving the problems identified during the discovery research.

I therefore created an interactive prototype that I have tested via usability testing.

I have used a rainbow chart to capture all errors that occurred during the test. The chart helped me to have an immediate visual overview on how often each issue came up during the sessions. See the full report with rainbow chart here

Issues were grouped in four main categories:

  • Terminology: some specific terms related to hormonal phases were not understood (luteal, follicular, ovulatory). Also the word “cycle” was misunderstood with “period”.

  • Graphs & Visual elements: certain visual elements showing trends and patterns in logs were not understood. (Graph showing hormonal changes during the cycle, graph comparing past symptoms logs).

  • Simplification: some task flows created unnecessary friction (e.g. the creation of a custom symptoms list before logging the symptoms).

  • Transparency: Information about pricing for telehealth services appears too late during the booking. Too little information is provided to decide which service to book.

Visual Design

After having validated my idea I selected colors, typography, and patterns to create a design style guide with instructions on how to apply those to the final user interface (UI). Color contrast and visual elements have been optimized to meet the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG).

The style guide also includes good practices for the tone of voice and inclusive language to be used throughout the design.

Reflections

This project challenged me to empathize with users while talking about very sensitive and private topics such as menstrual health and reproductive conditions.

I have also learned about inclusive language when it comes to talking about “women health”. I had to rethink my communication approach and use gender-neutral phrases and wording to include all individuals experiencing menstruation issues.

Through usability testing I learned that every extra step or screen in the flow could higher the probability of task failure or friction. Therefore ideating simple and logical flow to perform the target action is fundamental to ensure adoption. Color can be an important element to create a thematic connection between elements. However relying on color only is not a good accessibility practice. A mix of colors, text and symbols is what works best to ensure design accessibility.
